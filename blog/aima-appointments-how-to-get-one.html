AIMA Appointments in Portugal: Why They're Scarce and How to Get One

November 24, 2025  ·  9 min read  ·  Visa Process

Securing an AIMA appointment is, for many expats, the most frustrating part of the entire Portugal relocation process. You have your visa. You have your rental contract. You have every document in the packet, and you cannot get an appointment for weeks or months because slots disappear within minutes of becoming available.

This article explains what AIMA is, what happens at the appointment, why the system is under such strain, and, most importantly, practical strategies to actually secure a slot.

What Is AIMA?

AIMA stands for Agência para a Integração, Migrações e Asilo, the Agency for Integration, Migration and Asylum. It replaced SEF (Serviço de Estrangeiros e Fronteiras, the Foreigners and Borders Service) in October 2023 as Portugal's primary immigration authority.

The transition was intended to modernise the immigration system and shift enforcement functions to the police while focusing AIMA on integration and residence services. In practice, AIMA inherited SEF's substantial appointment backlog and has been working through it ever since.

What Happens at the AIMA Appointment?

The AIMA appointment is a required in-person meeting that converts your entry visa into a full Portuguese residence permit. During the appointment, an AIMA officer will:

  • Verify your identity and all submitted documents
  • Take your biometrics, fingerprints and a photograph
  • Confirm your eligibility under your specific visa category
  • Issue or initiate processing of your Autorização de Residência (Residence Authorisation card)

Without completing this appointment, you remain in a legal grey area, present on your entry visa but unable to obtain the full residence card that unlocks banking, SNS registration, social security, and formal work rights.

Why Are Appointments So Hard to Get?

The scarcity is structural. Portugal experienced a surge in immigration applications, from Americans, Brazilians, British nationals post-Brexit, and others, that far outpaced the system's processing capacity. AIMA inherited SEF's backlog at the same moment it was being restructured. Staffing levels have not kept pace with demand.

The result is a booking system where available slots are released in batches, often at midnight or early morning, and claimed within minutes, frequently by automated monitoring tools or people who set alarms. The experience of refreshing a page repeatedly while slots fill in real time is a shared frustration across Portugal's expat community.

The 120-Day Window

Important timing note: After entering Portugal on your long-stay visa, you typically have 120 days to schedule your AIMA appointment. This is not a suggestion, failing to act within this window can compromise your legal status. Start the booking process as early as possible after arrival.

The 120-day rule creates urgency. You cannot wait passively for a convenient slot to materialise, you need to be actively pursuing an appointment from day one.

How to Book an AIMA Appointment

There are two official channels:

  1. Online portal: Appointments can be booked at the AIMA online scheduling platform (the system has been updated, verify the current URL via the official AIMA website or your immigration lawyer). Slots typically release at midnight or early morning and fill within minutes.
  2. Phone: AIMA operates a central phone line for appointment booking. Wait times are typically long, and availability by phone is limited, but it is worth attempting as a parallel approach.

Practical Strategies That Actually Work

1. Use Automated Monitoring

The most effective approach is to use a tool that monitors the booking system and alerts you the moment a slot becomes available. Dora. VisaWise's AI advisor, can alert you when AIMA appointment slots open, giving you a significant advantage over manual page-refreshing.

2. Have All Documents Ready Before You Book

Do not waste a slot gathering paperwork. When a slot appears, you will have seconds to confirm the booking. Have every document compiled, organised, and ready to go before you begin actively monitoring for availability. A missed slot because you were not prepared is a frustrating and avoidable setback.

3. Work With a Lawyer or Concierge Service

Immigration lawyers and relocation concierge services in Portugal often have insight into when slots become available and may have direct contacts within the system. The cost of professional assistance frequently pays for itself in time saved and stress avoided.

4. Check Your Local Câmara Municipal

Some municipalities (Câmaras Municipais) have local arrangements for urgent cases or can facilitate appointments for residents facing imminent deadline issues. Contact your local câmara if you are approaching the 120-day limit without a confirmed appointment.

5. Set Alerts and Check at Odd Hours

Slots most commonly release in the early hours of the morning. If you are monitoring manually, set an alarm for midnight and again at 6 a.m. Persistence and timing matter.

What to Bring to Your AIMA Appointment

Arriving with an incomplete document set can result in your appointment being rescheduled, a significant setback given how hard slots are to obtain. Bring the following:

  • Valid passport (original + copy)
  • Entry visa (stamped in your passport)
  • 2 recent passport-size photographs
  • Proof of address, rental contract plus a recent utility bill
  • Proof of income, bank statements, employment contract, or pension letters
  • NIF (tax identification number)
  • Proof of health insurance
  • Completed application form (available on the AIMA website)
  • Payment for applicable fees (check current amounts on the AIMA website before your appointment)

Make photocopies of everything and bring both originals and copies.

After the Appointment: What to Expect

Once your appointment is complete and biometrics taken, processing typically takes 4–8 weeks. Your Cartão de Residência (Residence Card) will either be sent by post or collected in person at the AIMA office, you will be notified of which applies to your case.

The residence card is your formal proof of legal residency in Portugal. With it in hand, you can:

  • Register with the SNS public health system
  • Open a full Portuguese bank account without difficulty
  • Register for Social Security (Segurança Social)
  • Exercise formal employment rights
  • Travel within the Schengen Area with ease
